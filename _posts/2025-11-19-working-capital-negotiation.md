Working capital provisions can swing deal value by 20-30%. Here's what I'm learning:

## What Is Normal Working Capital (NWC)?
- Current assets minus current liabilities (excluding cash and debt)
- Typically: AR + Inventory - AP - Accrued Expenses
- "Normal" = average over past 12-24 months, adjusted for seasonality
- Seller delivers this level at close, or purchase price adjusts

## Why It Matters

**Scenario 1: Seller Delivers Less Than Normal WC**
- Business has $500K normal WC
- At close, only $350K actual WC (collected receivables, didn't restock inventory)
- Buyer pays $150K less, or seller funds the $150K difference
- Protects buyer from seller extracting working capital pre-close

**Scenario 2: Seasonal Business Complexity**
- Landscaping business: working capital spikes in spring/summer
- Purchase price adjustment needs to account for time of year
- Use normalized WC target, not point-in-time snapshot

## Common LOI Working Capital Structures

**Dollar Peg**
- "Seller to deliver $400K in working capital at close"
- Clear, simple, easy to calculate
- Requires agreement on what's "normal" upfront

**Floating Target**
- "Working capital to be delivered = average of prior 24 months"
- Calculated at close based on formula
- More flexible, less chance of gaming

**Range/Collar**
- "WC between $350-450K, no adjustment. Outside range = dollar-for-dollar adjustment"
- Reduces small adjustments and post-close disputes
- Common in deals <$5M where precision matters less

## Items I'm Learning to Negotiate

**Excluded from Working Capital**
- Cash (you're buying the business, not their cash reserves)
- Debt (handled separately in enterprise value calculation)
- Shareholder loans or related party receivables
- Often: prepaid expenses (depends on nature)

**Included (Usually)**
- Accounts receivable (at net realizable value)
- Inventory (at lower of cost or market)
- Accounts payable
- Accrued expenses (payroll, taxes, etc.)

## Seller Games to Watch

**Aggressive Collections Pre-Close**
- Offering steep discounts to collect AR quickly
- Damages customer relationships
- Leaves you with slow-paying customers only
- Mitigation: collar/cap on how much AR can decline

**Inventory Depletion**
- Running down inventory to generate cash
- You inherit empty shelves and backorders
- Mitigation: minimum inventory levels in LOI

**Stretching Payables**
- Delaying payments to suppliers beyond terms
- You inherit angry vendors and damaged relationships
- Mitigation: specify that AP should be within terms

## Buyer Protections in LOI

- Define working capital components specifically
- Set target based on historical average (show your math)
- Require monthly WC reporting between LOI and close
- Right to object to unusual changes in AR/AP/Inventory
- Post-close adjustment mechanism (usually within 30-90 days)
- Escrow or holdback for disputed amounts

## Real Example I'm Studying

$3M purchase price, $450K normal working capital:
- At close audit: $380K actual WC delivered
- Shortfall: $70K
- Adjustment: Buyer pays $2.93M instead of $3M, or
- Seller wires $70K to business after close
